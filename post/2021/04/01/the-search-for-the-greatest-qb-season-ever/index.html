The Search For the Greatest QB Season Ever

and it's Unexpected Challenger

Sam Metcalfe / 2021-04-01

The Buccaneers winning their championship in 2020 all but put away the debate over the greatest quarterback of all time. That has been settled. I found myself asking what the greatest season by an NFL quarterback was.

I assembled my list of what I believe to be the top 10 seasons in NFL history. This list was made up of Hall of Famers, Champions, and MVPs. Legends of the sport.

I compiled the season stats and dug in.

What did I find? An unlikely hero in Nick Foles’ 2013 campaign with the Philadelphia Eagles. Sound ridiculous? It should, but let’s get into some numbers.

The data I’ll be working with comes from Pro-Football Reference’s NFL database.

The first question that had to be asked was: what seasons are worthy to be included in this search? I compiled a selection from the top yard, touchdown, and QB rating seasons from a variety of QBs.

The roster is assembled, now let’s see what they got. Some quarterbacks on my list (Nick Foles, Aaron Rodgers) played less games, but all met my minimum requirements (at least 15 passes per team game played) to be considered for this study. Now the obvious stats to look at to grade a QBs season are passing yards and touchdowns, so that will be the first thing we look at.

With each QBs yards and touchdowns per game values, I made bar charts for comparison between the passers.

No surprises here, Peyton Manning’s 2013 season in Denver dominates the volume charts, throwing for the most yards per game, and racking up almost 4 touchdowns per outing. The NFL average is roughly 234 yards with 1.45 touchdowns per game, so while Manning may have shown out on this chart, all these quarterbacks blew those averages out of the water, certainly living up to the prestige of the best seasons of all time.

And don’t count out Nick Foles quite yet, volume is not where he separates himself.

The next metric I looked into was winning percentage. Quarterback is the most important position on the field, and therefore receive the most credit/blame for a game’s outcome.

To find this, because of the way the data is set up, we have to separate the wins and losses. Doing this is fairly simple, and we can separate the outcomes of games as well as calculate winning percentages using the following code chunks.

CleanQB <- QBdata %>% separate(QBrec, into=c("Wins", "Losses", "Ties"), sep="-") %>% 
    mutate(Wins = as.numeric(Wins), Losses = as.numeric(Losses))
WinPercent <- CleanQB %>% 
 mutate(
   winningpercentage = (Wins / GS)
  )

With this data set created, the winning percentages of each quarterback can be displayed in a lollipop chart. Tom Brady’s perfect 16-0 in 2007 certainly tops this list, but all of these quarterbacks led their teams to the playoffs, most with a first round bye. All but 1 of our seasons managed to see their teams win at least 75% of their games.

When it comes to winning percentage, Brady takes the cake, but it’s narrow when you consider the level of success all of these QBs were able to achieve.

Now is when things start to come together for Nick Foles. We’re going to start taking a look at what is arguably the most important trait in a quarterback: Ball Security

Using a scatterplot, we can factor in passer rating and interception percentage in order to find out which QBs on our roster were the smartest with the ball.

The seasons inside of the red circle are the best when it comes to ball security, having passer ratings over 115, and interception percentage below 1.49%. We see that Brady, Rodgers (x2), and Foles all fall within the circle. In 2013, Nick Foles threw just 0.6 interceptions for every hundred passes he threw. This is a dramatic decrease from the next closest, Aaron Rodgers (2020) throwing 1 pick for every hundred passes.

Among the greatest seasons of all time, Foles was nearly twice as careful with the football than the next closest QB.

The previous charts considered only a quarterback’s ability to move the ball down field OR protect the football over the course of the season. These are arguably the two most important traits for a QB, so I think the most telling chart of just how impressive a season is should consider both.

This chart allows us to see which passers moved the ball most effectively, while also factoring in the importance of protecting the football. The bubble size will also allow us to determine how many points the QBs were able to put on the board in each game.

This is where Nick Foles separates himself.

He averages the 3rd highest yards gained per pass attempt, at 9.1, only 1/10 of a yard behind the top 2. But what’s most impressive, is that he did so while throwing 13.5 touchdowns for every interception he threw. This is far and away the most touchdowns thrown per interception among our legendary seasons, and it was done while also moving the ball at the 3rd highest clip.

All things considered, it’s hard to go wrong naming any number of these seasons the greatest ever. Peyton Manning put up numbers that had never been seen before, Tom Brady won every game he played in, and Aaron Rodgers was, well, Aaron Rodgers. But Nick Foles may have a case that’s just as compelling, or more.

He may not be bound for Canton, but looking beyond the numbers he posted, and examining the way in which he did it, none of the other quarterbacks in this legendary set compare. In 2013, Nick Foles just may have had the most impressive season of all time.
